\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Expander graphs occupy a peculiar position in mathematics and theoretical computer science. They are sparse, in the sense that each vertex has only constantly many neighbors, yet they behave in many respects like complete graphs, transmitting information rapidly and mixing random walks to near-uniformity in logarithmically many steps. This combination of sparsity and connectivity, which is in principle an impossible tension to resolve, turns out to be achievable, and the explicit constructions of expanders~\cite{Mar1973,LPS1988,RVW2002} are among the most celebrated results in combinatorics and group theory.

The applications of expanders in theoretical computer science are extraordinarily broad. Pinsker~\cite{Pin1973} used superconcentrators built from expanders to design optimal sorting networks; subsequent work showed that expander codes yield asymptotically optimal error-correcting codes with linear-time encoding and decoding~\cite{GS1999}; Reingold~\cite{Rei2008} used the zig-zag product of expanders to prove that undirected $s$-$t$ connectivity is decidable in logarithmic space, resolving a decade-old open problem; and Impagliazzo and Wigderson~\cite{IW1997} used the random-walk mixing properties of expanders to construct pseudorandom generators under the assumption that $\mathsf{E}$ requires exponential circuits.

Cryptography draws on all of these threads. Expanders underlie constructions of randomness extractors~\cite{Vad2012}, hash functions with provable collision-resistance bounds~\cite{HLW2006}, pseudorandom generators from random walks on constant-degree graphs, and protocols for secure multiparty computation with sublinear communication complexity. In each case, the key property being exploited is the same, namely that a random walk on an expander converges to its stationary distribution exponentially fast, and that this convergence rate is governed by the spectral gap of the graph. The spectral gap is both a quantitative measure of expansion and a proxy for pseudorandomness, and the two interpretations are made precise by the expander mixing lemma and its consequences.

The goal of this note is to give a self-contained and mathematically careful introduction to expander graphs and their cryptographic applications. We proceed in four steps. We first define expansion and develop its combinatorial and spectral characterizations in \cref{sec:expander-graphs,sec:spectral-theory}, proving the discrete Cheeger inequality that relates the two. We then describe the main explicit constructions in \cref{sec:constructions}, with particular attention to the zig-zag product of Reingold, Vadhan, and Wigderson~\cite{RVW2002} and the algebraic constructions of Lubotzky, Phillips, and Sarnak~\cite{LPS1988}. Finally, in \cref{sec:cryptographic-applications} we survey the cryptographic applications, tracing in each case how the expansion property is translated into a security or efficiency guarantee. We assume familiarity with basic probability, linear algebra, and the rudiments of complexity theory at the level of~\cite{Gol2008}.

\parhead{Organization.} \Cref{sec:preliminaries} fixes notation and collects background on graphs and Markov chains. \Cref{sec:expander-graphs} defines expanders and proves the equivalence of the main combinatorial notions. \Cref{sec:spectral-theory} develops the spectral theory of the adjacency and normalized Laplacian matrices and states the expander mixing lemma. \Cref{sec:constructions} presents the main explicit constructions. \Cref{sec:cryptographic-applications} covers the cryptographic applications.
