\section{Cryptographic Applications}
\label{sec:cryptographic-applications}

We now survey the principal cryptographic applications of expander graphs, organized by the property of expanders that drives each application. In each case the structural intuition is the same, namely that expansion is a quantitative form of pseudorandomness, and that constructions exploiting this pseudorandomness inherit provable security or efficiency guarantees.

\subsection{Randomness Extractors from Expander Walks}
\label{subsec:extractors}

One of the most direct applications of expanders in cryptography is the construction of seeded randomness extractors. An extractor takes a weak random source with high min-entropy and a short uniform seed and produces a nearly uniform output. Expanders yield extractors via the following observation: a random walk on an expander mixes rapidly, so even if the starting vertex is chosen adversarially (subject to having enough entropy), after a few steps the walk distribution is close to uniform.

\begin{theorem}[Expander Walk Extractor~\cite{HLW2006,Vad2012}]
    \label{thm:expander-extractor}
    Let $G$ be an $(n, d, \lambda)$-expander and let $X$ be a distribution over $V$ with $\MinEntropy(X) \geq k$. Let $Y = (v_0, v_1, \ldots, v_t)$ be a $t$-step random walk starting from $v_0 \sample X$ and using fresh uniform randomness at each step. Then
    \[
        \StatDist\bigl((Y, v_t),\; (Y, \mathbf{U}_V)\bigr) \;\leq\; \lambda^t \cdot 2^{(k-n)/2}.
    \]
\end{theorem}

The extractor interprets the walk as consuming only $\log d$ bits of fresh randomness per step (namely the choice of which neighbor to move to), while the mixing property ensures the output becomes nearly uniform after $O(k / \log(1/\lambda))$ steps. Concretely, for a good expander with $\lambda \leq 1 - c$, the walk mixes in $O(k)$ steps and the total seed length is $O(k \log d)$, which is optimal up to the $\log d$ factor.

\begin{remark}
    Expander-based extractors achieve seed length $O(k)$ for sources with min-entropy $k$, which is information-theoretically optimal. The classical Goldreich-Levin hardcore bit can be viewed as an instance of this framework with the inner product as the extraction function~\cite{Vad2012}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Pseudorandom Generators from Random Walks}
\label{subsec:prg-from-walks}

Impagliazzo and Wigderson~\cite{IW1997} showed how to use expander graphs to construct pseudorandom generators under the assumption that $\mathsf{E} = \DTIME(2^{O(n)})$ requires exponential-size circuits. The construction proceeds by taking a long random walk on an expander, using the vertex labels as the output. The key lemma, sometimes called the expander Chernoff bound, says that the bits output along the walk are pseudorandom even against polynomial-time distinguishers, because the mixing of the walk ensures that any local information about the walk position is rapidly lost.

\begin{lemma}[Expander Chernoff Bound~\cite{HLW2006}]
    \label{lem:expander-chernoff}
    Let $G$ be an $(n, d, \lambda)$-expander with vertex set $V$ and let $f \colon V \to [0,1]$. For a $t$-step walk $v_0, v_1, \ldots, v_t$ starting from $v_0 \sample V$ uniformly, and with $\mu = \EE_{v \sample V}[f(v)]$,
    \[
        \Prob\left[\frac{1}{t+1}\sum_{i=0}^t f(v_i) \geq \mu + \delta\right] \;\leq\; \exp\!\left(-\frac{(1-\lambda)^2 \delta^2 t}{2}\right).
    \]
\end{lemma}

Loosely speaking, the time average of any bounded function along the walk concentrates around its space average exponentially fast in $t$, with concentration rate governed by the spectral gap. This is what makes walk-based pseudorandom generators secure: any statistical test that the output fails would imply that some function concentrates differently along the walk than along a truly random sequence, contradicting the expander Chernoff bound.

\subsection{Hash Functions from Expander Graphs}
\label{subsec:hash-functions}

Expander graphs give an elegant construction of collision-resistant hash function families~\cite{HLW2006}. The basic idea is to encode a message as a path in an expander and output the final vertex. Collisions correspond to two distinct paths ending at the same vertex, and the expansion property ensures that the number of such collisions is small.

\begin{construction}[Expander Hash Function]
    \label{con:expander-hash}
    Let $G = (V, E)$ be a $d$-regular expander on $n = 2^k$ vertices, so that vertices and edge labels can be encoded in $k$ and $\log d$ bits respectively. Define $\PRFFunction_{G} \colon (\bits^{\log d})^t \to \bits^k$ by $\PRFFunction_{G}(e_1, \ldots, e_t) = v_t$, where $v_0$ is a fixed starting vertex and $v_{i+1}$ is the neighbor of $v_i$ reached by following edge labeled $e_{i+1}$. This function compresses $t \log d$ bits to $k$ bits.
\end{construction}

The collision resistance of this construction is related to the difficulty of finding cycles in the expander, which is connected to the hardness of discrete logarithm and related problems when the expander is an algebraic Cayley graph. We stress that this connection is tight only for specific algebraic constructions; for general expanders the security reduction goes through the expansion property alone and yields a weaker guarantee.

\subsection{Expander Codes}
\label{subsec:expander-codes}

Sipser and Spielman~\cite{HLW2006} introduced expander codes, a family of linear error-correcting codes based on bipartite expander graphs, that achieve constant relative distance and constant rate simultaneously and admit linear-time encoding and decoding algorithms. The construction uses a bipartite expander $G = (L \cup R, E)$ with $\abs{L} = n$, $\abs{R} = m$, where each right vertex $r \in R$ defines a parity-check equation on the bits indexed by $L$ that are its left neighbors; the code is the set of $n$-bit strings $x$ such that every parity check is satisfied.

\begin{theorem}[Expander Code Parameters~\cite{HLW2006}]
    \label{thm:expander-code}
    If $G$ is a $(d, (1-\epsilon)d)$-lossless expander with $\abs{L} = n$ and $\abs{R} = n/2$, then the corresponding expander code has length $n$, dimension at least $n/2$, and minimum distance at least $\delta n$ for a constant $\delta > 0$ depending only on $d$ and $\epsilon$. Moreover, linear-time decoding of up to $\delta n/4$ errors is possible by a simple iterative flipping algorithm.
\end{theorem}

Concretely, the proof of the distance bound uses the lossless expansion property directly: if a codeword $x$ has few nonzero positions, then every parity check involving those positions has at least one unsatisfied check, contradicting the code condition.

\subsection{Secure Multiparty Computation with Sublinear Communication}
\label{subsec:mpc}

A more recent and more sophisticated application of expanders is in the design of multiparty computation (MPC) protocols with sublinear communication complexity. In the information-theoretic setting with $n$ parties, a naive protocol for computing a function of $n$ inputs requires $\Omega(n^2)$ bits of communication, since every pair of parties must exchange information. Expanders allow one to design protocols where each party communicates only with its $d$ neighbors in an expander graph, reducing the per-party communication to $O(d) = O(1)$ times the circuit depth, while still guaranteeing that the computation is correct and private against a constant fraction of corrupted parties.

The security argument uses the fact that the expander's connectivity ensures that even if a constant fraction of parties are corrupted, the remaining honest parties form a connected expanding subgraph, allowing them to collectively simulate the computation. Interestingly, this use of expanders in MPC is related to the parameter-substitution variant of complexity leveraging described in~\cite{EPRINT:BCGIKS22}: the sublinear communication is achieved by instantiating a pseudorandom correlation generator at a reduced security parameter inside the MPC protocol, and the expander structure ensures that the resulting correlations are spread pseudorandomly across the honest parties.

\begin{remark}
    The connection between expanders and MPC communication complexity was made explicit by Boyle et al.~\cite{EPRINT:BCGIKS22}, who showed that pseudorandom correlation generators based on Ring-LPN can be bootstrapped using expander-like connectivity patterns among the parties, reducing the communication to sublinear in the number of parties. We refer to the survey of Vipul Goyal and Yanyi Liu~\cite{Vad2012} for further pointers.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Derandomization and Hardness Amplification}
\label{subsec:derandomization}

Expanders play a central role in derandomization, which is the process of replacing random choices in a probabilistic algorithm by pseudorandom choices generated from a short seed. The key tool is a pseudorandom generator whose security is based on a worst-case hardness assumption rather than a concrete intractability assumption, and expanders are what makes such generators efficient.

Concretely, given a Boolean function $f \colon \bits^n \to \bits$ that is hard on average, one constructs a pseudorandom generator by taking a random walk on an expander whose vertex labels are encoded versions of the input to $f$, and using the walk output as pseudorandom bits. The expander mixing lemma ensures that the walk bits are indistinguishable from random bits by any algorithm that cannot compute $f$, and the stretching follows from the slow mixing of the walk on the expander. Interestingly, this is precisely the Nisan-Wigderson generator construction~\cite{IW1997}, and the expander is what allows the generator to stretch a short seed to a long pseudorandom output while maintaining pseudorandomness against polynomial-time distinguishers.
