\begin{abstract}
    Expander graphs are sparse yet highly connected graphs whose remarkable combinatorial and spectral properties make them indispensable throughout theoretical computer science and, in particular, throughout cryptography. In this note, we give a self-contained introduction to expander graphs and their role in cryptographic constructions. We define expansion through three equivalent lenses, namely combinatorial edge expansion, vertex expansion, and spectral gap, and trace the connections between them via the discrete Cheeger inequality. We then survey the main cryptographic applications, covering randomness extractors and condensers, pseudorandom generators from random walks, hash functions with provable collision resistance, error-correcting codes, and protocols for secure computation with sublinear communication. Throughout, we emphasize the structural intuition that expansion is essentially a quantitative form of mixing, and that constructions exploiting this mixing inherit strong pseudorandomness and fault-tolerance properties that would be difficult or impossible to achieve with arbitrary sparse graphs.
\end{abstract}
